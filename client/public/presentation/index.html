§ I — Problem
Three actors, one missing handoff
01 · The problem

Three people touch the bill. None of them check the note against the code.

Step 01
Physician
  • Writes the clinical note
  • Often via Doximity / AI scribe
  • Documents for clinical accuracy, not billing
▶
Step 02
Billing Company
  • Pulls the note from the EMR
  • Picks the CPT & ICD-10 codes
  • Downcodes when the note is unclear — 5–15% revenue lost
▶
Step 03
Insurance Payer
  • 71% use AI for utilization mgmt
  • Auto-downcodes E/M levels
  • Only 11.7% of denials are appealed
Doximity writes the note. The billing company picks the codes. Payers use AI to deny. Nobody checks whether the note supports the codes.

§ VI — Build
What's actually under the hood
07 · The MVP

Five engines, one defensibility report.

01
Defensibility analysis
Compares note evidence to the requirements of the selected CPT / ICD-10 codes. Returns a score, gaps, and fix suggestions.
02
Code suggestion (coder mode)
Upload a note without pre-selected codes; get the highest defensible CPT & ICD-10 with confidence levels.
03
E/M leveling
CMS 2021 MDM rules applied as deterministic logic; AI extracts elements, code decides the level.
04
Financial impact
Per-note dollar lift using Medicare PFS rates we manually pulled and labelled; commercial rates flagged separately.
05
Payer downcoding monitor
Deterministic rules per payer (Medicare · UHC · Aetna · BCBS · Cigna), refreshed weekly by an n8n workflow.
JWT auth · custom domain docdefend.health · Vercel + Render · 10 sample notes (6 PM + 4 FM) · synthetic only

§ VIII — On AI
What worked · part 2 of 4
10 · What worked

Where AI made us faster than we had any business being.

i
A four-person team shipped a full-stack MVP in 14 weeks.

Across React, Express, the Anthropic SDK, JWT auth, file upload, PDF export — most of which none of us would have written by hand in time.

ii
Domain expertise in the prompt beat reaching for a bigger model.

The unlock wasn't a bigger model — it was the team's clinical knowledge encoded as system prompts and grounding data: Amir's hospitalist-scribe years plus the operating experience the rest of us bring inside healthcare.

iii
Structured JSON output turned a chatty model into a reliable API.

Every engine returns a typed shape the front-end can trust. The model is a function call, not a conversation.

iv
Shipping in rings, not phases.

Defensibility, downcoding, financial impact, coder mode — each was independently demoable. AI lets you split scope finer than you used to.

§ VIII — On AI
Where it broke · part 3 of 4
11 · Where it broke

The four cracks we learned to build around.

i
AI couldn't get us a single interview.

Cold outreach to physicians (15-20) and to billing companies (8) returned zero replies across two semesters of attempts. Personal networks did all the work.

ii
The model was confidently wrong about coding rules.

Hallucinated CPT requirements with full conviction. Fix: the downcoding engine is deterministic on purpose — rules from CMS / payer policy, no AI in the path.

iii
AI couldn't tell us who the customer was.

Three interviews pointed three different directions (provider · billing co · neither). Synthesis is a human job; the model can't do it for you.

iv
Token economics compound at scale.

$0.04 per note feels free. At 200 notes / provider / week across a vertical, the API line becomes the COGS conversation. Caching isn't optional.

§ VIII — On AI
What we'd tell ourselves on day one · part 4 of 4
12 · What we wish we'd known in January

The four things we'd put in week one's CLAUDE.md if we did this again.

i
Write the spec before asking the model to build it.

The hours we lost were the hours we hadn't put pen to paper first. The model writes to a spec; without one, you're paying for confident guesses.

ii
Talk to a real user in week one.

AI made building so cheap that validation became the bottleneck. A 30-minute call in week 1 would have saved us two features in week 6.

iii
Pick a wedge, not a platform.

AI tempts you to build everything. The discipline is choosing the smallest version that one specific person will pay for, and resisting the rest.

iv
Distribution is the moat.

Anyone can ship our app now. The defensible asset is the relationship with 20 clinics and the credibility of having sat in the room.

§ IX — On Entrepreneurship
What doing it taught us
13 · Entrepreneurship · first-hand

Six lessons that came from doing the thing, not reading about it.

i.
The build is the easy part. Selling, validating, picking the right customer — that's where the real semester happened.
ii.
Cold outreach is brutal. Warm intros are everything. Two rounds of cold outreach (35+ contacts) = zero replies. Every interview we got came through a warm hand-off.
iii.
Every advisor disagrees with every other advisor. Ranga: pivot. Rodrigo: pricing. Jordan: switch buyers. Shivani: switch back. Synthesis is the founder's job, not theirs.
iv.
Founder-market fit is the strongest pitch we have. Between Amir's years inside the EMR as a hospitalist scribe and the team's clinical contacts and operating experience in healthcare, we know the workflow we're fixing — and we know the people who use it.
v.
Tool fatigue is real. The tool that wins fits inside an existing workflow. "If AI is already writing my notes, why would I need another AI to check them?" — and that's the buyer we want.
vi.
A pivot is data, not a failure of conviction. Each of our four pivots followed a specific conversation we can name. The semester moved when we let the data speak.
